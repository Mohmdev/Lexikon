# Why adjectives grow endings

## The situation

After "to be," the adjective is **bare**:

> Das Auto ist **schnell**. — The car is fast.

Move it in front of the noun and it suddenly grows a tail — and the tail isn't even always the same:

> das **schnelle** Auto — the fast car
> ein **schnelles** Auto — a fast car

Same word *schnell*, same noun, two different endings (-e, -es). Beginners see this and assume it's
chaos. It isn't. There's **one rule** underneath all of it.

---

## The one rule: the phrase wears its badge exactly once

A German noun phrase has to show **which case and which gender** it is. Picture it as a single badge
the phrase must wear. The badge can be worn by the **article** *or* by the **adjective** — but the
phrase only needs it shown **once**. So there's a hand-off depending on who's free to wear it.

**The article already shows it clearly →** the adjective relaxes.
*der* is obviously masculine, *das* is obviously neuter. The work is done, so the adjective takes a
lazy, almost-empty ending — just **-e** or **-en**.
> der **schnelle** Wagen — the fast car
These are the **weak** endings (file 02).

**No article at all →** the adjective must become the badge itself.
With nothing in front, the adjective steps up and takes a **strong** ending that looks like the
article would have looked:
> **kaltes** Wasser — cold water
That **-es** is the *das*-badge ("das Wasser") moved onto the adjective. These are the **strong**
endings (file 04).

**A half-helpful article (ein) →** the adjective covers only the gaps.
*ein* is lazy — by itself it can't tell you masculine from neuter (*ein* fits both *der Wagen* and
*das Auto*). So the adjective fills in exactly what *ein* failed to show:
> ein **schneller** Wagen — a fast car *(adjective shows masculine)*
> ein **schnelles** Auto — a fast car *(adjective shows neuter)*
These are the **mixed** endings (file 03).

---

## That's the whole system

Three tables, **one idea**: the case/gender badge appears once per phrase, and whoever is free wears
it. When you're ever unsure of an ending, ask yourself:

> *Did the article already show the case and gender?*
> **Yes** → the adjective is lazy: -e / -en.
> **No** → the adjective wears the article's badge: the strong, article-looking ending.

The exact endings are in the next three files. This page is just the *why* — and once you see the
hand-off, those three tables aren't three things to memorize, they're three views of one rule.
