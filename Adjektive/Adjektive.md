# Adjektive (adjectives) — start here

An adjective is a describing word for a **thing**: groß (big), gut (good), schnell (fast). You've
already got a pile of them in [[Adjektive 001-100]]. This folder is the **grammar** — the small
set of rules for actually using them.

The good news: there's really only **one** hard thing (endings), and it sits in the middle of a clean
staircase. Here's the whole climb, bottom to top.

---

## 0. The free win — after "to be," no ending

When the adjective comes after **sein / werden / bleiben** (to be / become / stay), it stays **bare**,
exactly as it sits in the dictionary. No ending, no change.

> Das Auto ist **schnell**. — The car is fast.
> Der Mann wird **alt**. — The man is getting old.

This alone lets you describe almost anything. Start here and you're already productive on day one.

## 1. Why endings show up at all

The moment the adjective moves in **front** of the noun, it grows an ending:

> das **schnelle** Auto — the fast car

Before any tables, [[01-why-endings]] explains the single idea behind every
ending. Learn this one thing and the rest stops feeling random.

## 2–4. The endings, easiest to hardest

The ending depends on **what sits before the adjective**. Three situations, three small tables:

- after **der / die / das** → [[02-weak]] — the easy one (only -e or -en)
- after **ein / kein / mein** → [[03-mixed]]
- **no article at all** → [[04-strong]] — the adjective carries the load

These lean on already knowing the **four cases** and the **der/die/das table**. That base grammar
lives elsewhere — come back to 02–04 once it's solid.

## 5. Comparison — bigger, biggest

A separate, lighter topic: schnell → schneller → am schnellsten, plus **so … wie** (as … as) and
**als** (than). [[05-comparison]].

## 6. Turning an adjective into a noun

etwas **Gutes** (something good), der **Deutsche** (the German man). A common German trick worth
knowing early. [[06-adjective-as-noun]].
