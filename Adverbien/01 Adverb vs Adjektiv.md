# Adverb vs Adjektiv — same word, two jobs

In German an adverb is almost identical to an adjective.

## Two targets for a describing word

> a **fast** car → "fast" describes a **thing** (the car). That's an **adjective**.
> he drives **fast** → "fast" describes an **action** (the driving). That's an **adverb**.

Same idea — "fastness" — aimed at two different targets. **Adjective points at a noun; adverb points
at a verb.** That is the whole distinction.

## English changes the word, German does not

When English switches from describing a thing to describing an action, it often **changes the word**:

| describing a thing (adjective) | describing an action (adverb) | changed? |
|---|---|---|
| a **fast** car | he drives **fast** | no |
| a **quick** car | he drives **quickly** | yes — added **-ly** |
| **good** food | she cooks **well** | yes — whole new word |

German does none of that. The plain adjective is also the adverb. **No -ly, no second word.**

| German word | as adjective (thing) | as adverb (action) |
|---|---|---|
| **schnell** | das **schnelle** Auto (the fast car) | das Auto fährt **schnell** (drives fast) |
| **gut** | **gutes** Essen (good food) | sie kocht **gut** (she cooks well) |
| **leise** | die **leise** Musik (quiet music) | sprich **leise** (speak quietly) |

Where English needs a new word ("well"), German reuses **gut**.

## The only difference: endings

As an **adjective in front of a noun**, the word takes an ending (schnell**e**, gut**es**). As an
**adverb**, it stays **bare** (schnell, gut):

- in front of a noun (adjective) → takes an ending → see [[Adjektive]]
- describing a verb (adverb) → stays **bare**, no ending, ever

**Adverbs never change form:** no gender, no case, no plural, no endings. Their difficulty is not in
their shape but in **where they sit in the sentence** (files 03–04).

## Manner adverbs are stored as adjectives

Learn the adjective *schnell = fast* and you also have the adverb *schnell = quickly*. Manner adverbs
that are also adjectives are not collected twice; they live in [[Adjektive]], doing a second job.

## Exception: adverbs that are not adjectives

Some words are adverbs only and cannot stand in front of a noun. They have no entry in Adjektive, so
they are collected here:

> **gern** (gladly), **leider** (unfortunately), **hoffentlich** (hopefully), **vielleicht** (maybe),
> **so** (like this), **anders** (differently)

> Ich trinke **gern** Tee. — I gladly drink tea. *(but never "ein gern Tee" — gern can't describe a noun)*

These adverbs are sorted by meaning in the next file, [[02 Adverbtypen]].
