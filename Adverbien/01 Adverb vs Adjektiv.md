# Adverb vs Adjektiv — same word, two jobs

Before anything else, clear up what an adverb even *is* — because in German it's almost the same thing
as an adjective, and that's good news for you.

## A describing word can point at two different targets

> a **fast** car → "fast" describes a **thing** (the car). That's an **adjective**.
> he drives **fast** → "fast" describes an **action** (the driving). That's an **adverb**.

Same idea — "fastness" — aimed at two different targets. **Adjective points at a noun; adverb points
at a verb.** That's the whole distinction.

## English is messy here. German is clean.

Watch English when you switch from describing a thing to describing an action — it often **changes the
word**:

| describing a thing (adjective) | describing an action (adverb) | changed? |
|---|---|---|
| a **fast** car | he drives **fast** | no |
| a **quick** car | he drives **quickly** | yes — added **-ly** |
| **good** food | she cooks **well** | yes — whole new word |

German does **none** of that. The plain adjective is *automatically* also the adverb. **No -ly, no
second word ever.**

| German word | as adjective (thing) | as adverb (action) |
|---|---|---|
| **schnell** | das **schnelle** Auto (the fast car) | das Auto fährt **schnell** (drives fast) |
| **gut** | **gutes** Essen (good food) | sie kocht **gut** (she cooks well) |
| **leise** | die **leise** Musik (quiet music) | sprich **leise** (speak quietly) |

Where English needs a new word ("well"), German just reuses **gut**.

## The only visible difference: endings

Look closely at the two columns. As an **adjective in front of a noun**, the word grew an ending
(schnell**e**, gut**es**). As an **adverb**, it's **bare** (schnell, gut):

- in front of a noun (adjective) → takes an ending → that's the [[Adjektive]] machinery
- describing a verb (adverb) → stays **bare**, no ending, ever

That bareness is the headline of this whole folder: **adverbs never change form.** No gender, no case,
no plural, no endings. Whatever difficulty adverbs have is *not* in their shape — it's in **where they
sit in the sentence** (that's files 03–04).

## The payoff

Learn the adjective *schnell = fast* and you've **already** got the adverb *schnell = quickly* — for
free. So manner adverbs that are really adjectives aren't collected twice; they live in [[Adjektive]],
doing a second job.

## The exception: real adverbs that are NOT adjectives

A handful of words are adverbs *only* — you can't stick them in front of a noun. These have no home in
Adjektive, so they're collected here:

> **gern** (gladly), **leider** (unfortunately), **hoffentlich** (hopefully), **vielleicht** (maybe),
> **so** (like this), **anders** (differently)

> Ich trinke **gern** Tee. — I gladly drink tea. *(but never "ein gern Tee" — gern can't describe a noun)*

These genuine adverbs are sorted by meaning in the next file, [[02 Adverbtypen]].
