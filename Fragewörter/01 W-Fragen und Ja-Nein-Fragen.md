# W-Fragen und Ja-Nein-Fragen — two question types

German has **two question types**. They differ only in **what sits in front of the verb**.

> In **both** types the **conjugated verb comes early** (slot 1 or 2). German marks a question by
> **fronting the verb**, not by intonation alone.

---

## 1. Ja-Nein-Fragen — verb first

A yes/no question has **no question word**. Move the conjugated verb to the **front** (slot 1), with
the subject directly after it:

> **Kommst** du morgen? — Are you coming tomorrow?
> **Hast** du Zeit? — Do you have time?
> **Ist** das dein Auto? — Is that your car?

There is **no German "do"** — German uses no helper like English *do/does*. The main verb itself
moves to the front. Answer with **ja / nein** (or **doch** — see below).

---

## 2. W-Fragen — question word first, verb second

An open question starts with a **W-word** (wer, was, wann, wo, wie, warum…) in **slot 1**; the verb
follows in **slot 2**. This is the standard verb-second rule, with the W-word in the front field:

> **Wann** kommst du? — When are you coming?
> **Was** machst du? — What are you doing?
> **Warum** lachst du? — Why are you laughing?

Pattern: **[W-word] [verb] [subject] …** The full inventory of W-words → [[02 Die Fragewörter]].

---

## Patterns compared

| type | slot 1 | slot 2 | rest |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ja/Nein** | **verb** | subject | … |
| **W-Frage** | **W-word** | **verb** | subject … |

In both, the verb stays near the front. This is the question case of the general verb-second rule →
[[02 Word Order]].

---

## Questions about a preposition object

If a verb takes a preposition (*warten **auf**, sprechen **mit**, denken **an***), the **preposition
moves to the front with the question word** — German does not strand it at the end as English does:

- **about a person** → preposition + **wen/wem**:
  > **Mit wem** sprichst du? — Who are you talking **with**? *(lit. "with whom")*
  > **Auf wen** wartest du? — Who are you waiting **for**?
- **about a thing** → fuse it into **wo(r)- + preposition** (worauf, womit…):
  > **Worauf** wartest du? — What are you waiting for?

The thing-vs-person split is the question side of [[07 Pronominaladverbien]]; the case on *wen/wem*
comes from [[03 wer wen wem wessen]].

---

## doch — answering a negative question

If a question contains a **negative**, "yes, actually" is **doch**, not *ja*. **doch** has no direct
English equivalent:

> **Kommst du nicht?** — Aren't you coming?
> — **Doch!** — Yes (I am)! *(contradicting the negative)*
> — **Nein.** — No (I'm not). *(confirming it)*

---

## Summary

Two types: **yes/no → verb first**; **open → W-word first, verb second**. No German "do"; the
preposition moves to the front with the question word; **doch** contradicts a negative question.
→ the W-words themselves: [[02 Die Fragewörter]]
