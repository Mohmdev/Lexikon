# Artikel (articles) — start here

An article is the little word before a noun — *the* dog, *a* dog. English has two (*the*, *a*) and
they never change. German's articles are the **workhorse of the noun phrase**: they carry the noun's
**gender** and its **case** so the noun itself barely has to move ([[01 Kasus]]). Get the article
right and most of the grammar around a noun falls into place.

Two families:

- **Definite** — "the" → *der / die / das*. → [[01 Bestimmte]]
- **Indefinite** — "a / an" → *ein / eine*, plus its negative *kein* ("no / not a"). → [[02 Unbestimmte]]

---

## The big compression: it's really just **two** patterns

Here's the payoff that makes this whole corner of German cheap. There are a *lot* of little
determiner words — *the, a, this, every, which, no, my, your…* — but they all copy **one of two
ending-patterns**:

| family | base | also copied by | grid |
| ------ | ---- | -------------- | ---- |
| **der-words** | der / die / das | **dieser** (this), **jeder** (every), **welcher** (which), **mancher** (some), **aller** (all) | [[01 Bestimmte]] |
| **ein-words** | ein / eine | **kein** (no), and the possessives **mein, dein, sein, ihr, unser, euer** | [[02 Unbestimmte]] |

So you don't learn a dozen separate words — you learn **two grids**, then plug each new determiner
into the family it belongs to. *dieser/diese/dieses* is just *der/die/das* endings on a *dies-* stem;
*mein/meine/mein* is just *ein/eine/ein* endings on a *mein-* stem.

(The two grids are *almost* the same — they differ in exactly **three cells**, which is the whole
story of [[02 Unbestimmte]].)

---

## Knowing the shapes isn't enough — you also need *when*

The two grids tell you the *shape* of each article. A separate skill is choosing **which one to use, or
none at all** — and German disagrees with English in both directions (*Ich bin **Arzt*** with no
article; ***Der** Hund ist treu* with an extra one). That usage is its own page: [[03 Artikelgebrauch]].

## Why articles matter beyond themselves

- They're how **case** becomes visible — *der → den → dem → des* shows the job the noun is doing,
  even when the noun doesn't change.
- They decide the **adjective endings** that follow them — a different article means a different
  adjective ending ([[Adjektive]]).
- The der-words and ein-words above are exactly the **possessives** ([[02 Possessive]]) and
  **demonstratives** ([[05 Demonstrative]]) you meet over in [[Pronomen]] — same grids, different home.

Learn these two tables well; they pay rent across the entire language.
