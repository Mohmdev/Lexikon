## 3. Compounding

German forms long words by joining shorter ones, and the meaning is **literal**. Split a compound
and you can read it:

> **Hand**schuh = hand-shoe = **glove**
> **Kranken**haus = sick-house = **hospital**
> **Flug**hafen = flight-harbor = **airport**
> **Fern**seher = far-seer = **TV**

A long word is usually three known words combined. For how compounds inherit gender and plural, see
[[03 Komposita]].

## 4. Spelling matches pronunciation

German is **read as written**. Once you know the sound of each letter and combination
(*sch, ei, ie, Ă¤/Ă¶/ĂĽ*), you can pronounce any word on sight: no silent letters.

## False friends

Some words look English but mean something else. Common examples:

| German | looks like | actually means |
|---|---|---|
| **bekommen** | become | to **get / receive** |
| **das Gift** | gift | **poison** |
| **also** | also | **so / therefore** |
| **der Chef** | chef | **boss** |
| **das Handy** | handy | **cell phone** |
| **eventuell** | eventually | **possibly / maybe** |

The cognate patterns hold in most cases, but when a word seems too convenient in context,
verify it. The patterns are reliable, not absolute.
