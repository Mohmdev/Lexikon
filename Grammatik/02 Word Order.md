# Wortstellung (word order) — verb position

German word order is governed mainly by **the position of the conjugated verb**. The rules below
cover its placement in each clause type.

---

## 1. Main clause: verb second (V2)

In a statement, the conjugated verb is the **second element**, regardless of what comes first. An
"element" is one chunk (a subject, a time word, a whole phrase), not one word.

> **Ich** *gehe* heute nach Hause. — I'm going home today.
> **Heute** *gehe* **ich** nach Hause. — Today I'm going home.
> **Nach Hause** *gehe* **ich** heute. — Home is where I'm going today.

Whatever fills slot 1, the verb *gehe* stays in slot 2 and the subject follows it. English keeps
subject-then-verb; German keeps **verb-second**.

---

## 2. Questions: verb first or verb second

Two types, both variants of the V2 rule:

- **Yes/no question → verb first:** drop the front element and lead with the verb.
  > Du **kommst** heute. → **Kommst** du heute? — Are you coming today?
- **W-question → W-word, then verb second:** the question word (*wer, was, wann, wo, wie, warum…*)
  fills slot 1, so the verb stays in slot 2.
  > **Wann kommst** du? — When are you coming? · **Was machst** du? — What are you doing?

The question words themselves live in [[Fragewörter]].

---

## 3. Subordinate clause: verb at the end

After a connector like **weil** (because), **dass** (that), **wenn** (if/when), **ob** (whether), the
conjugated verb moves to **the very end**:

> Ich bleibe zu Hause, **weil** ich müde **bin**. — I'm staying home because I'm tired.
> Ich weiß, **dass** er morgen **kommt**. — I know that he's coming tomorrow.

A subordinate clause opened by such a connector requires its conjugated verb in final position.
(Which connectors trigger this, and which do not, is in [[Konjunktionen]].)

---

## 4. The sentence bracket (Satzklammer)

When a verb has **two parts** — a modal + infinitive, a perfect tense (haben/sein + participle), or a
separable verb — the conjugated part takes slot 2 and the **other part drops to the end**, wrapping
the sentence:

> Ich **will** heute nach Hause **gehen**. — I want to go home today. *(modal)*
> Ich **habe** den Ball **gekauft**. — I have bought the ball. *(perfect)*
> Ich **rufe** dich morgen **an**. — I'll call you tomorrow. *(separable verb)*

Everything else sits *inside* the bracket — **will … gehen**, **habe … gekauft**, **rufe … an**.

---

## 5. Position of nicht

To negate a whole sentence, **nicht** comes **late** — at the end, but *before* any stranded verb
part or a predicate adjective:

> Ich kenne den Mann **nicht**. — I don't know the man. *(end)*
> Ich kann heute **nicht** kommen. — I can't come today. *(before the infinitive)*
> Das ist **nicht** gut. — That's not good. *(before the adjective)*

To negate just one element, put **nicht** right in front of it: *Ich fahre **nicht** heute* (not
today). For negating a noun, you usually use **kein** instead → [[02 Unbestimmte]].

---

## The middle field

When several time/manner/place elements fill the middle zone, they order as **TeKaMoLo** (time →
cause → manner → place). That rule is covered with the adverbs: [[04 TeKaMoLo]].
