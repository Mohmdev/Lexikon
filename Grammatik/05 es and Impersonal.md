# es & impersonal constructions

German uses **es** ("it") far more than English — often as a **placeholder subject** where there is no
real subject. The high-frequency cases below are best learned as fixed patterns.

---

## es gibt — "there is / there are"

**es gibt** + **Akkusativ** means "there is/are." It **never changes** for number —
always *es gibt*, singular, whether one thing or many:

> **Es gibt** einen Park in der Stadt. — There's a park in the city.
> **Es gibt** viele Leute hier. — There are many people here.
> **Gibt es** ein Problem? — Is there a problem?

Note the object is **Akkusativ** (*einen* Park), because *geben* takes a direct object — even though
in English "there is" feels like a subject.

---

## es for weather, time, and wellbeing

Weather and time have no real subject, so *es* fills the slot:

> **Es** regnet. — It's raining. · **Es** schneit. — It's snowing. · **Es ist** kalt. — It's cold.
> **Es ist** drei Uhr. — It's three o'clock.

**es geht** expresses wellbeing (with a Dativ experiencer):

> Wie **geht es** dir? — How are you? *(lit. "how goes it to you?")*
> **Es geht** mir gut. — I'm doing well.

---

## es as a dummy placeholder in slot 1

*es* can hold the first slot ([[02 Word Order]]) so the real subject comes later, for
flow or emphasis. The verb still agrees with the **real** subject, not with *es*:

> **Es** kommen viele Gäste. — Many guests are coming. *(real subject = viele Gäste → plural verb)*
> **Es** war einmal ein König… — Once upon a time there was a king…

Dropping the *es* and reordering also works: *Viele Gäste kommen.* The *es* is a placeholder only.

---

## Dative experiencer

In this related impersonal pattern the person affected goes in the **Dativ**, and there is often no real
subject:

> **Mir** ist kalt. — I'm cold. *(lit. "to me is cold")*
> **Mir** ist schlecht. — I feel sick.
> **Es** gefällt **mir**. — I like it. *(lit. "it pleases to me" — gefallen takes Dativ, [[01 Kasus]])*
