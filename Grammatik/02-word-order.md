# Word order — where the verb goes

German word order is mostly about **one thing: where the conjugated verb sits.** Nail that and the
rest follows. There are three rules.

---

## 1. Main clause: the verb is ALWAYS second (V2)

In a normal statement, the conjugated verb is the **second element** — no matter what comes first.
"Element" means one chunk (a subject, a time word, a whole phrase), not one word.

> **Ich** *gehe* heute nach Hause. — I'm going home today.
> **Heute** *gehe* **ich** nach Hause. — Today I'm going home.
> **Nach Hause** *gehe* **ich** heute. — Home is where I'm going today.

See it? Whatever you put in slot 1, the verb *gehe* stays glued to slot 2, and the subject slides
behind it. This is the rule English speakers break most — English keeps subject-then-verb; German
keeps **verb-second** above all.

## 2. Subordinate clause: the verb goes to the END

After a connector like **weil** (because), **dass** (that), **wenn** (if/when), **ob** (whether), the
conjugated verb is **kicked to the very end**:

> Ich bleibe zu Hause, **weil** ich müde **bin**. — I'm staying home because I'm tired.
> Ich weiß, **dass** er morgen **kommt**. — I know that he's coming tomorrow.

This is your "closing bracket" instinct: open a subordinate clause, and you owe a verb at the end.

## 3. The sentence bracket (Satzklammer)

When a verb has **two parts** — a modal + an infinitive, or a perfect tense (haben/sein + participle)
— the conjugated part takes slot 2 and the **other part drops to the end**, wrapping the sentence:

> Ich **will** heute nach Hause **gehen**. — I want to go home today.
> Ich **habe** den Ball **gekauft**. — I have bought the ball.

Everything else lives *inside* the bracket **will … gehen** / **habe … gekauft**. German builds the
frame first and fills the middle — exactly the nested structure you noticed.
