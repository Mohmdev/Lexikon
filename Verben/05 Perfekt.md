# Perfekt — the everyday (spoken) past

This is **the** past tense for conversation. When a German talks about yesterday, they almost always
use the Perfekt — for *every* verb, not just some. The simple past ([[06 Präteritum]]) exists, but in
speech it's reserved for a handful of verbs; everything else goes Perfekt.

The shape mirrors English's "I **have** eaten" — a helper verb plus a participle — but German uses it
where English would just say "I ate" too:

> Ich **habe** Pizza **gegessen**. — I ate / I have eaten pizza.
> Ich **bin** nach Hause **gegangen**. — I went home.

---

## The formula

> **haben** or **sein** (conjugated, present) + **Partizip II** (at the very end)

The helper takes the normal verb slot (second); the participle drops to the end. Same **bracket** as
the modals — the two verb parts wrap the sentence ([[02 Word Order]]):

> Ich **habe** gestern einen Film **gesehen**. — I watched a film yesterday.

Two things to get right: **how to build the participle**, and **which helper** to use.

---

## Building the Partizip II

This is just [[04 Schwache und starke Verben]] applied. The participle is the verb's third principal part:

- **Weak:** **ge-** + stem + **-t** → machen → ge**macht**, spielen → ge**spielt**
- **Strong:** **ge-** + stem + **-en**, often a new vowel → singen → ge**sungen**, gehen → ge**gangen**

Three groups **skip the ge-** prefix:

- **-ieren verbs** → studieren → **studiert**, telefonieren → **telefoniert**
- **inseparable-prefix verbs** (be-, ver-, er-, ent-…) → besuchen → **besucht**, verkaufen →
  **verkauft** ([[08 Trennbare Verben]])
- **separable-prefix verbs** tuck the ge- **in the middle**, behind the prefix → aufstehen →
  **auf**ge**standen**, einkaufen → **ein**ge**kauft**

---

## haben or sein?

**haben is the default** — use it unless the verb qualifies for *sein*. A verb takes **sein** when it
means:

- **motion from A to B** — gehen, fahren, kommen, fliegen, laufen
  > Ich **bin** nach Berlin **gefahren**. — I drove to Berlin.
- **a change of state** — werden (become), sterben (die), aufwachen (wake up), aufstehen (get up)
  > Er **ist** eingeschlafen. — He fell asleep.
- plus the two odd-ones-out: **sein** itself and **bleiben** (stay)
  > Ich **bin** zu Hause **geblieben**. — I stayed home.

Everything else takes **haben** — including all verbs with a direct object:

> Ich **habe** das Auto **gewaschen**. — I washed the car.
> Wir **haben** gut **gegessen**. — We ate well.

Rough intuition: if the subject **goes somewhere** or **changes into a new state**, it's *sein*;
if it **does something to something**, it's *haben*. This helper choice is part of the verb's
fingerprint, so it's stored with each verb in [[Verben 001-100]] (the *ist gegangen* in the triple).

## A few worked examples

> **Hast** du das **gehört**? — Did you hear that? *(haben + weak)*
> Sie **hat** mir geholfen. — She helped me. *(haben + strong, geholfen)*
> Wir **sind** spät **angekommen**. — We arrived late. *(sein, motion + separable: an-ge-kommen)*
> Ich **habe** Deutsch **gelernt**. — I learned German. *(haben + weak)*
