# Imperativ — giving commands

A German command drops the subject and leads with the verb. The verb form depends on the addressee:
German has three forms of "you" (*du / ihr / Sie*), plus a "let's" form for *wir*.

---

## The four forms

| addressee | how to build it | *kommen* → | *machen* → |
| --------- | --------------- | ---------- | ---------- |
| **du** (one person, informal) | the bare **stem** (drop -st) | **Komm!** | **Mach!** |
| **ihr** (a group, informal) | = the present **ihr**-form | **Kommt!** | **Macht!** |
| **Sie** (formal, any number) | verb + **Sie** (verb first) | **Kommen Sie!** | **Machen Sie!** |
| **wir** ("let's…") | verb + **wir** (verb first) | **Kommen wir!** | **Machen wir!** |

> **Komm** her! — Come here! *(to a friend)*
> **Kommen Sie** bitte herein. — Please come in. *(formal)*
> **Gehen wir!** — Let's go!

The *du*-form sometimes adds an optional **-e** (*Komm* or *Komme*); in speech the bare form is far
more common.

---

## Stem-change verbs

From [[01 Präsens]]: some verbs change their vowel in the *du*-form. In the imperative:

- **e → i / ie** changes **stay**: geben → du gibst → **Gib!** · lesen → du liest → **Lies!**
- **a → ä** changes **drop**: fahren → du fährst → **Fahr!** (not *Fähr!*) · schlafen → **Schlaf!**

---

## sein is irregular

Memorize these forms:

> **Sei** ruhig! — Be quiet! *(du)*
> **Seid** ruhig! — *(ihr)*
> **Seien Sie** ruhig! — *(Sie)*

---

## Separable verbs still split

The prefix goes to the end, exactly as everywhere else ([[08 Trennbare Verben]]):

> **Steh** auf! — Get up! · **Ruf** mich **an**! — Call me! · **Kommt** mit! — Come along!

---

## Softening the order

A bare imperative can sound blunt. German softens it with **bitte** (please) and the tone particles
*mal* / *doch* ([[Partikeln]]):

> **Komm mal** her. — Come here (for a sec). *(casual, friendly)*
> **Mach** das **bitte**. — Please do that.
> **Setz dich doch!** — Oh, do sit down! *(warm, inviting)*
