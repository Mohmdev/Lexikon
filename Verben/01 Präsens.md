# Präsens — the stem & the present tense

Every German verb in the dictionary is an **infinitive**: *machen* (to do), *spielen* (to play),
*gehen* (to go). Chop off the **-en** and what's left is the **stem** — the workbench everything
bolts onto:

> mach**en** → **mach-** · spiel**en** → **spiel-** · geh**en** → **geh-**

To conjugate (= match the verb to its subject), you glue a **personal ending** onto that stem. The
endings are a tiny closed set — six slots, four distinct endings — so you learn them **once** and can
run almost any verb.

---

## The ending table

| person | ending | *machen* | English |
| ------ | ------ | -------- | ------- |
| ich (I) | **-e** | mach**e** | I do |
| du (you, one person) | **-st** | mach**st** | you do |
| er / sie / es (he/she/it) | **-t** | mach**t** | he does |
| wir (we) | **-en** | mach**en** | we do |
| ihr (you, a group) | **-t** | mach**t** | you do |
| sie / Sie (they / you-formal) | **-en** | mach**en** | they do |

Notice the recycling: **wir** and **sie/Sie** are identical (-en), and **er** and **ihr** are
identical (-t). That's why it's really four endings to hold, not six.

---

## Free win: one German present = two English presents

German has **no separate "-ing" tense**. *Ich mache* covers both English presents at once:

> Ich **mache** das. — I **do** that. *AND* I **am doing** that.

So you never have to choose between "I do" and "I am doing" — German doesn't draw the line. One form,
both meanings.

---

## Two spelling adjustments (so it stays pronounceable)

Not really new rules — just the stem refusing to be unsayable:

**1. Stem ends in -d or -t → slip an -e- in** before the -st and -t endings (otherwise *du arbeitst*
is a tongue-twister):

> arbeiten → du arbeit**e**st · er arbeit**e**t · ihr arbeit**e**t
> finden → du find**e**st · er find**e**t

**2. Stem already ends in an s-sound (-s, -ss, -ß, -z) → the du-ending drops its s.** The stem
already hisses, so -st collapses to just -t:

> heißen → du heiß**t** *(not heißst)* · tanzen → du tanz**t** · lesen → du lie**st**

---

## Stem-change verbs — the vowel flips in *du* and *er* only

A big group of common verbs **change their stem vowel** — but *only* in the **du** and
**er/sie/es** forms. Everywhere else (ich, wir, ihr, sie) the stem stays plain. Three patterns:

| change | infinitive | du / er form | English |
| ------ | ---------- | ------------ | ------- |
| **a → ä** | fahren | du f**ä**hrst · er f**ä**hrt | drive |
| | schlafen | er schl**ä**ft | sleep |
| **e → i** | geben | du g**i**bst · er g**i**bt | give |
| | sprechen | er spr**i**cht | speak |
| | essen | du **i**sst · er **i**sst | eat |
| **e → ie** | sehen | du s**ie**hst · er s**ie**ht | see |
| | lesen | er l**ie**st | read |

> Ich **fahre** nach Berlin, aber er **fährt** nach Köln. — I'm driving to Berlin, but he's driving to Cologne.

See how only *er* flips? *ich fahre*, *wir fahren*, *ihr fahrt* keep the plain **a**.

A few have to be memorized whole — *nehmen* (to take) also doubles a consonant: *du nimmst, er
nimmt*.

**You can't predict which verbs do this from the infinitive** — it's per-verb data. This vowel-flip
is the visible tip of the **strong** verbs ([[04 Schwache und starke Verben]]); the change gets stored with each
verb in [[Verben 001-100]].
