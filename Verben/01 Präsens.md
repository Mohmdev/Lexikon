# Präsens — stem and present tense

Every German verb in the dictionary is an **infinitive**: *machen* (to do), *spielen* (to play),
*gehen* (to go). Remove the **-en** and what remains is the **stem**, to which all endings attach:

> mach**en** → **mach-** · spiel**en** → **spiel-** · geh**en** → **geh-**

To conjugate (match the verb to its subject), attach a **personal ending** to the stem. The endings
are a closed set: six slots, four distinct endings, which apply to almost any verb.

---

## The ending table

| person | ending | *machen* | English |
| ------ | ------ | -------- | ------- |
| ich (I) | **-e** | mach**e** | I do |
| du (you, one person) | **-st** | mach**st** | you do |
| er / sie / es (he/she/it) | **-t** | mach**t** | he does |
| wir (we) | **-en** | mach**en** | we do |
| ihr (you, a group) | **-t** | mach**t** | you do |
| sie / Sie (they / you-formal) | **-en** | mach**en** | they do |

Two pairs share an ending: **wir** and **sie/Sie** both take -en, and **er** and **ihr** both take
-t. This reduces the six slots to four distinct endings.

---

## One German present covers two English presents

German has **no separate "-ing" tense**. *Ich mache* covers both English presents:

> Ich **mache** das. — I **do** that. *AND* I **am doing** that.

German does not distinguish "I do" from "I am doing": one form, both meanings.

---

## Two spelling adjustments

Both keep the form pronounceable:

**1. Stem ends in -d or -t: insert -e-** before the -st and -t endings (*du arbeitst* would be
unpronounceable):

> arbeiten → du arbeit**e**st · er arbeit**e**t · ihr arbeit**e**t
> finden → du find**e**st · er find**e**t

**2. Stem ends in an s-sound (-s, -ss, -ß, -z): the du-ending drops its s.** Since the stem already
ends in a sibilant, -st reduces to -t:

> heißen → du heiß**t** *(not heißst)* · tanzen → du tanz**t** · lesen → du lie**st**

---

## Stem-change verbs — the vowel flips in *du* and *er* only

A large group of common verbs **change their stem vowel**, but *only* in the **du** and
**er/sie/es** forms. Elsewhere (ich, wir, ihr, sie) the stem stays plain. Three patterns:

| change | infinitive | du / er form | English |
| ------ | ---------- | ------------ | ------- |
| **a → ä** | fahren | du f**ä**hrst · er f**ä**hrt | drive |
| | schlafen | er schl**ä**ft | sleep |
| **e → i** | geben | du g**i**bst · er g**i**bt | give |
| | sprechen | er spr**i**cht | speak |
| | essen | du **i**sst · er **i**sst | eat |
| **e → ie** | sehen | du s**ie**hst · er s**ie**ht | see |
| | lesen | er l**ie**st | read |

> Ich **fahre** nach Berlin, aber er **fährt** nach Köln. — I'm driving to Berlin, but he's driving to Cologne.

Only *er* changes; *ich fahre*, *wir fahren*, *ihr fahrt* keep the plain **a**.

A few are irregular beyond the vowel change: *nehmen* (to take) also doubles a consonant: *du
nimmst, er nimmt*.

**Which verbs change cannot be predicted from the infinitive**: it is per-verb data. The vowel
change marks the **strong** verbs ([[04 Schwache und starke Verben]]); it is stored with each verb
in [[Verben 001-100]].
