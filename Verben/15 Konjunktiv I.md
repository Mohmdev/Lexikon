# Konjunktiv I — reported speech

German uses Konjunktiv I for **indirect (reported) speech** — mainly in journalism and formal
writing — to repeat a statement while staying neutral about whether it is true.

> Direct: Er sagt: **„Ich bin krank."** — He says, "I'm sick."
> Reported: Er sagt, er **sei** krank. — He says he's sick.

The switch from *ist* to **sei** marks the statement as reported, not asserted by the speaker.

> This is mostly a **receptive** skill: recognize it when reading news. In speech, Germans usually use
> the indicative (*er sagt, dass er krank ist*) or Konjunktiv II. Learn to read it, and produce the few
> key forms.

---

## How it's formed

From the **infinitive stem + -e, -est, -e, -en, -et, -en**. In practice you mostly see the **3rd
person singular** (*er/sie/es*), which is where it's clearest:

| verb | Konjunktiv I (er/sie/es) |
|---|---|
| **sein** | **sei** *(fully irregular)* |
| **haben** | **habe** |
| **werden** | **werde** |
| **können** | **könne** |
| **müssen** | **müsse** |
| gehen / kommen / machen | **gehe / komme / mache** |

> Sie sagt, sie **habe** keine Zeit. — She says she has no time.
> Er meint, er **könne** das nicht. — He claims he can't do it.

*sein* is the key verb: its forms (sei, seien) are distinct and frequent: *er sei, sie seien.*

---

## Ambiguous forms: fall back to Konjunktiv II

Konjunktiv I is often **identical** to the ordinary present, so it signals nothing. Where the form is
ambiguous, German **falls back to [[14 Konjunktiv II]]**:

> *sie gehen* (K I) = *sie gehen* (indicative) → ambiguous → use **sie gingen / würden gehen**
> *ich habe* (K I) = *ich habe* (indicative) → ambiguous → use **ich hätte**

Practical rule: **use Konjunktiv I where it's visibly different (mostly *er/sie/es*: er sei, er habe,
er müsse); switch to Konjunktiv II everywhere it would clash with the indicative.**

---

## Tense in reported speech

Reported speech collapses time into three shapes:

| the original was… | reported as | example |
|---|---|---|
| present | K I present | Er sagt, er **sei** müde. |
| **any past** | K I perfect (habe/sei + P2) | Er sagt, er **sei** **gegangen** / er **habe** es **gemacht**. |
| future | werde + infinitive | Er sagt, er **werde** kommen. |

So all past tenses collapse into one reported form: **habe/sei + Partizip II.**

---

## Word order

Like other reported speech, you can use **dass** (verb to the end) or drop it (verb second):

> Er sagt, **dass** er krank **sei**. = Er sagt, er **sei** krank. *(see [[Konjunktionen]])*

---

## Priority for the learner

The main use is **reading**, not production. At B1, report speech with indicative + *dass* or with
Konjunktiv II. When a news article says *„Der Minister sagte, die Lage sei ernst,"* the *sei* marks the
statement as the minister's, not the paper's.
