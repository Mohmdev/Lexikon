# Konjunktiv I — reporting what someone else said

This is the **"those are their words, not my claim"** mood. German uses it for **indirect (reported)
speech** — mainly in journalism and formal writing — to repeat a statement while staying neutral about
whether it's true.

> Direct: Er sagt: **„Ich bin krank."** — He says, "I'm sick."
> Reported: Er sagt, er **sei** krank. — He says he's sick. *(I'm just relaying it)*

That little switch from *ist* to **sei** is the signal: *don't blame me, I'm only reporting.*

> ⚠️ This is mostly a **receptive** skill — you need to *recognize* it when reading news. In speech,
> Germans often just use the indicative (*er sagt, dass er krank ist*) or Konjunktiv II. So: learn to
> read it, and produce the few key forms if you want to sound educated.

---

## How it's formed

From the **infinitive stem + -e, -est, -e, -en, -et, -en**. In practice you mostly see the **3rd
person singular** (*er/sie/es*), which is where it's clearest:

| verb | Konjunktiv I (er/sie/es) |
|---|---|
| **sein** | **sei** *(the star — fully irregular)* |
| **haben** | **habe** |
| **werden** | **werde** |
| **können** | **könne** |
| **müssen** | **müsse** |
| gehen / kommen / machen | **gehe / komme / mache** |

> Sie sagt, sie **habe** keine Zeit. — She says she has no time.
> Er meint, er **könne** das nicht. — He claims he can't do it.

Note *sein* is the one to really know — its forms (sei, seien) are distinct and everywhere: *er sei,
sie seien.*

---

## The catch: when K I looks like normal present → use Konjunktiv II

Often Konjunktiv I is **identical** to the ordinary present, so it wouldn't signal anything. The fix:
where the form is ambiguous, German **falls back to [[14 Konjunktiv II]]**:

> *sie gehen* (K I) = *sie gehen* (indicative) → ambiguous → use **sie gingen / würden gehen**
> *ich habe* (K I) = *ich habe* (indicative) → ambiguous → use **ich hätte**

Practical rule: **use Konjunktiv I where it's visibly different (mostly *er/sie/es*: er sei, er habe,
er müsse); switch to Konjunktiv II everywhere it would clash with the indicative.**

---

## Tense in reported speech

Reported speech collapses time into three shapes:

| the original was… | reported as | example |
|---|---|---|
| present | K I present | Er sagt, er **sei** müde. |
| **any past** | K I perfect (habe/sei + P2) | Er sagt, er **sei** **gegangen** / er **habe** es **gemacht**. |
| future | werde + infinitive | Er sagt, er **werde** kommen. |

So all past tenses collapse into one reported form: **habe/sei + Partizip II.**

---

## Word order

Like other reported speech, you can use **dass** (verb to the end) or drop it (verb second):

> Er sagt, **dass** er krank **sei**. = Er sagt, er **sei** krank. *(see [[Konjunktionen]])*

---

## The honest take

Don't drill this for production now — at B1 you'll report speech fine with indicative + *dass* or with
Konjunktiv II. Its real job for you today is **reading**: when a news article says *„Der Minister sagte,
die Lage sei ernst,"* you now know the *sei* means "he said it — the paper isn't vouching for it."
