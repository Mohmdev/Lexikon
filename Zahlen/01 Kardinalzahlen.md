# Kardinalzahlen — the counting numbers (a generator, not a list)

This is the most **computable** corner of the whole language. You do **not** memorize a hundred words.
You memorize about **thirteen roots + a few assembly rules**, and they generate *every* number up to a
million. Learn the machine, not the output.

> **number = roots + build rules**

---

## Step 1 — the irregular core: 0–12 (memorize)

These don't follow any pattern. They're the seeds everything else is built from, so grind them cold:

| # | | # | | # | |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | null | 5 | fünf | 10 | zehn |
| 1 | eins | 6 | sechs | 11 | elf |
| 2 | zwei | 7 | sieben | 12 | zwölf |
| 3 | drei | 8 | acht | | |
| 4 | vier | 9 | neun | | |

*(Tip: on the phone Germans often say **zwo** for 2, to keep it from sounding like *drei*.)*

---

## Step 2 — 13–19: unit **+ zehn**

Glue the unit (3–9) onto *zehn*. Two tiny spelling dents:

> **drei**zehn 13 · **vier**zehn 14 · **fünf**zehn 15 · **sech**zehn 16 *(sechs drops the -s)* ·
> **sieb**zehn 17 *(sieben drops the -en)* · **acht**zehn 18 · **neun**zehn 19

---

## Step 3 — the tens: unit **+ -zig**

Same idea with *-zig* — plus four irregulars worth flagging:

> **zwan**zig 20 *(not "zwei-")* · **drei**ß**ig** 30 *(-ßig, not -zig!)* · **vier**zig 40 ·
> **fünf**zig 50 · **sech**zig 60 *(drops -s)* · **sieb**zig 70 *(drops -en)* · **acht**zig 80 ·
> **neun**zig 90

So: **20, 30, 60, 70** are the dents (zwanzig, dreißig, sechzig, siebzig); 40/50/80/90 are clean.

---

## Step 4 — 21–99: the famous backwards build

This is the German quirk everyone trips on. For any two-digit number, you say the **unit first**, then
**und**, then the **ten** — and write it as **one word**:

> **ein**und**zwanzig** — 21 *(literally "one-**and**-twenty")*
> **drei**und**dreißig** — 33 · **sieben**und**vierzig** — 47 · **neun**und**neunzig** — 99

Note **ein**, not *eins*, inside the compound (the -s drops): *einundzwanzig*, not *einsundzwanzig*.

Your brain has to **hold the small digit and say it first** — *"five-and-ninety"* for 95
(*fünfundneunzig*). This stays awkward for a while; it's pure practice.

---

## Step 5 — hundreds and thousands

| word | value | |
|---|---|---|
| **(ein)hundert** | 100 | *ein-* optional at exactly 100 |
| **(ein)tausend** | 1 000 | |
| zwei**hundert** | 200 | unit + hundert |
| drei**tausend** | 3 000 | unit + tausend |

Then you just **stack the parts in reading order** (hundreds → tens-unit), still as **one giant word**:

> 234 = **zwei**hundert**vier**und**dreißig** *(200 + [4-and-30])*
> 1 234 = **ein**tausend**zwei**hundert**vier**und**dreißig**

Reading trick: chop off *…tausend* and *…hundert* first, and remember the **last two digits flip**
(unit-und-ten). That flip is the only hard part of any big number.

---

## Million and up — these are **nouns**, not number-words

From a million on, the words become **feminine nouns**: capitalized, written **separately**, and taking
a plural **-en**:

> eine **Million** (1 000 000) · zwei **Millionen** · eine **Milliarde** (10⁹) · eine **Billion** (10¹²)

> ⚠️ **False-friend trap:** German **Milliarde** = English *billion* (10⁹). German **Billion** = English
> *trillion* (10¹²). Off by a thousand — be careful with big money/stats.

> 3 500 000 = **drei Millionen fünfhunderttausend** *(Millionen is a separate word; the rest glues up)*

---

## eins vs. ein — the one that shifts shape

- **eins** = the bare number, when counting or standing alone: *eins, zwei, drei…*; *Nummer eins.*
- **ein** before a noun (it behaves like the article): *ein Buch* — and inside compounds: *einundzwanzig*,
  *einhundert*.

---

## Cardinals don't decline (except *ein*)

Counting numbers are **invariable** — *zwei, drei, vier* never change for case or gender:

> **zwei** Häuser · mit **drei** Freunden · für **vier** Personen

The single exception is **ein**, because it's also the indefinite article and declines like one
(*ein/eine/einen…*). Full story with the undeclinable adjectives → [[10 Undeklinierbare Adjektive]].

---

## Writing numbers: German punctuation is flipped

- Thousands grouped with a **dot** or a space, **never** a comma: **1.000** or **1 000** (= one thousand).
- The decimal mark is a **comma** → see [[05 Bruchzahlen und Rechnen]].

So *1,000* to a German reader means **one point zero zero zero**, i.e. just *one*. This bites everyone.

---

## The shape in one line

Memorize **0–12**, the **sech-/sieb-** dents and **zwanzig/dreißig**, and **two build rules**
(unit+zehn/zig; unit-**und**-ten, one word). Stack hundreds and thousands in front, treat **Million+**
as nouns, and you can say every number there is. → ordinals next: [[02 Ordinalzahlen]]
