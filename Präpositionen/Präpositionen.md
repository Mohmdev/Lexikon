# Präpositionen — start here

## Präposition + Kasus = one unit

In English a preposition is just a meaning (*with, for, from*). In German a preposition **also forces a
case** on the noun behind it — so you never learn *mit* = "with" alone, you learn **mit + Dativ**.

> **mit** dem Hund — with the dog *(mit forces Dativ → dem)*
> **für** den Hund — for the dog *(für forces Akkusativ → den)*

Same dog, different article (dem / den), purely because the preposition demanded a different case. The
case itself is explained in [[01 Kasus]].

## The four groups (sorted by the case they force)

| Group | forces | list |
|---|---|---|
| Akkusativpräpositionen | always Akkusativ | [[01 Akkusativ]] |
| Dativpräpositionen | always Dativ | [[02 Dativ]] |
| Wechselpräpositionen | Akkusativ *or* Dativ (by meaning) | [[03 Wechsel]] |
| Genitivpräpositionen | always Genitiv (formal) | [[04 Genitiv]] |

So "which case?" is answered the moment you know **which group** the preposition is in.

## Two ways to slice the same words

The four files above sort prepositions by **case** — that's the *grammar* question (what ending does
the noun get?). But when you're actually speaking, you think in **meaning**: *when?* *where to?* The
same prepositions, re-sorted by job:

| you want to say… | go to | covers |
|---|---|---|
| **when** something happens | [[05 Temporal]] | am/im/um, vor/seit/bis, and the no-preposition trap |
| **to / from** a place | [[06 Lokal]] | the nach / zu / in tangle, aus vs von |
| a verb's **fixed** preposition | [[07 Verben mit Präposition]] | warten *auf*, denken *an* (Rektion) |

Use the case files to get endings right; use these to pick the right word in the first place.

## The honest part: which preposition takes which case is hardcode

There's no deep logic to *why* **für** is Akkusativ and **mit** is Dativ — it just **is**. This is a
**closed list** (a few dozen words total), so the move is to **grind the membership once** and own it
forever. The standard trick is to memorize each group as a **chant**:

- **Akkusativ:** *durch – für – gegen – ohne – um* (the core five)
- **Dativ:** *aus – außer – bei – mit – nach – seit – von – zu* (the classic eight)

Learn which bucket a preposition lives in, and the case falls out automatically. The one group with
*actual* logic is **Wechsel**, where the case flips with meaning — that's where to spend real thought
→ [[03 Wechsel]].

## Kontraktionen (contractions) — preposition + article fused

German glues common preposition + article pairs into one word. These are the normal, expected form —
not slang:

| full | fused | | full | fused |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| an dem | **am** | | zu dem | **zum** |
| in dem | **im** | | zu der | **zur** |
| an das | **ans** | | bei dem | **beim** |
| in das | **ins** | | von dem | **vom** |

> Ich gehe **zum** Arzt. — I'm going to the doctor. *(zum = zu dem)*
> Wir sind **im** Kino. — We're at the cinema. *(im = in dem)*

## One more layer: verbs that demand a preposition

Some prepositions aren't chosen by *you* but **dictated by a verb** — *warten **auf*** (wait for),
*denken **an*** (think of), *sich freuen **auf*** (look forward to). Here the preposition often loses
its literal meaning and just comes attached to the verb, with a fixed case. The phenomenon is taught in
[[07 Verben mit Präposition]]; the per-verb fingerprints are stored with each verb in [[Verben 001-100]].
