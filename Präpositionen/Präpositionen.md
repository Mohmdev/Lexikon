# Präpositionen

## Präposition + Kasus as one unit

In English a preposition carries only meaning (*with, for, from*). In German a preposition **also forces a
case** on the noun behind it, so *mit* is not learned alone but as **mit + Dativ**.

> **mit** dem Hund — with the dog *(mit forces Dativ → dem)*
> **für** den Hund — for the dog *(für forces Akkusativ → den)*

The same noun takes a different article (dem / den) solely because the preposition demands a different
case. The cases themselves are explained in [[01 Kasus]].

## The four groups by case

| Group | forces | list |
|---|---|---|
| Akkusativpräpositionen | always Akkusativ | [[01 Akkusativ]] |
| Dativpräpositionen | always Dativ | [[02 Dativ]] |
| Wechselpräpositionen | Akkusativ *or* Dativ (by meaning) | [[03 Wechsel]] |
| Genitivpräpositionen | always Genitiv (formal) | [[04 Genitiv]] |

The case is determined by **which group** the preposition belongs to.

## Two indexes: by case and by meaning

The four files above sort prepositions by **case** — the grammar question (what ending does the noun
get?). When speaking, the relevant question is **meaning**: *when?* *where to?* The same prepositions,
re-sorted by function:

| to express | go to | covers |
|---|---|---|
| **when** something happens | [[05 Temporal]] | am/im/um, vor/seit/bis, and the no-preposition case |
| **to / from** a place | [[06 Lokal]] | nach / zu / in, aus vs von |
| a verb's **fixed** preposition | [[07 Verben mit Präposition]] | warten *auf*, denken *an* (Rektion) |

Use the case files for endings; use these to choose the right word.

## Case assignment is fixed by membership

There is no rule for *why* **für** takes Akkusativ and **mit** takes Dativ; it is fixed. This is a
**closed list** (a few dozen words), so memorize group membership once. The standard method is to learn
each group as a fixed sequence:

- **Akkusativ:** *durch – für – gegen – ohne – um* (the core five)
- **Dativ:** *aus – außer – bei – mit – nach – seit – von – zu* (the classic eight)

Once the group is known, the case follows automatically. The one group governed by meaning is
**Wechsel**, where the case flips with the intended sense → [[03 Wechsel]].

## Kontraktionen (contractions) — preposition + article fused

German fuses common preposition + article pairs into one word. These are the standard form, not slang:

| full | fused | | full | fused |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| an dem | **am** | | zu dem | **zum** |
| in dem | **im** | | zu der | **zur** |
| an das | **ans** | | bei dem | **beim** |
| in das | **ins** | | von dem | **vom** |

> Ich gehe **zum** Arzt. — I'm going to the doctor. *(zum = zu dem)*
> Wir sind **im** Kino. — We're at the cinema. *(im = in dem)*

## Verbs that govern a preposition

Some prepositions are not chosen by meaning but **fixed by a verb** — *warten **auf*** (wait for),
*denken **an*** (think of), *sich freuen **auf*** (look forward to). Here the preposition often loses
its literal meaning and is bound to the verb with a fixed case. This is covered in
[[07 Verben mit Präposition]]; the per-verb details are stored with each verb in [[Verben 001-100]].
