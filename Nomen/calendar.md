## Time words — the pattern behind days, months & seasons

The **three rules** that make using days, months, seasons easy — and they're more regular than almost anything else in German.

### 1. They are ALL masculine — *der*

Every day, every month, every season is **der**. No exceptions, nothing to memorize per word.

> der Montag — Monday · der Januar — January · der Sommer — summer

See a day, month, or season → the article is *der*. Done.

### 2. Saying "on" / "in" a time → *am* / *im*

This drops straight out of the preposition rule (*an* / *in* for time take Dativ; masculine *dem*
fuses into *am* / *im*):

- **am** (= an dem) + a **day**
  > Ich komme **am Montag**. — I'm coming on Monday.
  > **am Wochenende** — on the weekend
- **im** (= in dem) + a **month** or **season**
  > Ich habe **im Januar** Geburtstag. — My birthday is in January.
  > **im Sommer** — in summer

Quick test — answer *Wann?* (when?): a **day** takes *am*, a **month/season** takes *im*.

> Wann hast du Geburtstag? — When's your birthday?
> **Im Mai**. — In May.

⚠️ One that breaks the pattern: "at night" is **in der Nacht**, not "im" — because *die Nacht* is
feminine, not masculine.

### 3. "every Monday" → lowercase it and add *-s*

Take a day, make it **lowercase**, add **-s**, and it turns into an adverb meaning "on ~s / every ~":

| noun (capital) | adverb (lowercase + -s) | meaning |
|---|---|---|
| der Montag | montags | on Mondays |
| der Sonntag | sonntags | on Sundays |

The same trick works on parts of the day:

> morgens — in the mornings · abends — in the evenings · nachts — at night
> Ich arbeite **sonntags** nicht. — I don't work on Sundays.

This is really a **time adverb built from a noun**, so it also belongs to the adverbs world —
see [Adverbien/Temporaladverbien.md](../Adverbien/Temporaladverbien.md).

---

That's the whole thing: **der + am/im + lowercase-s**. Three rules, no exceptions worth worrying about.
