# Nomen (nouns) — start here

A noun names a **thing** — a person, object, place, or idea: *Mann, Tisch, Stadt, Freiheit*. Nouns
are where German charges its biggest **per-word tax**: every single one comes with a **gender** and a
**plural** you can't fully predict. That's the whole challenge of this folder — and the habit that
beats it.

---

## Two free wins first

- **Every noun is capitalized** — *der **Hund**, die **Freiheit**, das **Auto***. Not just names —
  *all* of them. This is a gift when reading: a capital letter mid-sentence almost always means
  "noun starts here."
- **In the plural, the article is always *die*** — gender stops mattering once a noun goes plural
  (*der Tisch / die Lampe / das Haus* → all **die**).

---

## The one habit that decides everything: learn the triple

A noun's gender and plural are mostly **hardcode** — raw facts, not rules. The cheap fix is to encode
them **the first time you meet the word**, as a three-part unit:

> **article + singular + plural** → *das Haus, die Häuser*

Encoding gender + plural at first contact costs almost nothing. **Retrofitting** them onto thousands
of nouns later is exactly what stalls people at B1. So never learn a noun naked. The word list is
built around this triple → [[Nomen 001-100]].

---

## The pieces, foundational-first

### 1. Gender — der / die / das

The master fact: it drives articles, adjective endings, and pronouns. Mostly hardcode, but a set of
**reliable ending-rules** (-ung → die, -chen → das…) hands you a lot of it for free.
→ [[01 Genus]]

### 2. Plural — the five patterns

German makes plurals five different ways (-e, -en, -er, -s, -∅), semi-predictable from gender and
ending. The second half of the triple.
→ [[02 Plural]]

### 3. Compounds — gluing nouns together

*Hand + Schuh → Handschuh.* The payoff rule: the **last** noun sets the gender and plural for the
whole word, so a scary long compound inherits its grammar from its tail.
→ [[03 Komposita]]

### 4. Cases on the noun

Case mostly shows on the article, not the noun — but there are exactly **three** spots where the noun
itself takes an ending (Genitiv -s, Dativ-plural -n, and the n-declension).
→ [[04 Deklination]]

### 5. Wortbildung — building nouns (and getting gender free)

Most nouns aren't memorized — they're **built** from a verb or adjective + a standard suffix, and each
suffix *also* fixes the gender. *wohnen → die Wohnung, schön → die Schönheit, der Hund → das Hündchen.*
This is how you multiply vocabulary and skip half the gender tax at once.
→ [[05 Wortbildung]]
