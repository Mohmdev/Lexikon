# Komposita (compound nouns)

German builds new nouns by **gluing existing ones together**, and the meaning is usually literal — so
a terrifying long word is often just two or three words you already know stuck in a trench coat
([[03 Free Wins]]):

> **Hand** + **Schuh** → der Handschuh — "hand-shoe" = glove
> **Haus** + **Tür** → die Haustür — "house-door" = front door

This note is the **grammar** of that gluing — the one rule that saves you real work.

---

## The rule: the LAST noun is the boss

In a compound, the **final noun is the head**. It decides two things:

- the **gender** (the article), and
- the **plural**.

Everything before it is just a **modifier** describing it. So you don't need to learn a compound's
gender or plural separately — you **inherit** them from the last element, which you probably already
know:

| compound | last element | → gender & plural |
| -------- | ------------ | ----------------- |
| die Haus**tür** | die Tür | die Haustür → die Haustüren |
| das Garten**haus** | das Haus | das Gartenhaus → die Gartenhäuser |
| der Hand**schuh** | der Schuh | der Handschuh → die Handschuhe |

> *der* Saft + *die* Flasche → **die** Saftflasche *(feminine, because Flasche wins)*

This is a genuine **compute win**: read the word right-to-left, find the tail, and its grammar is the
whole compound's grammar.

---

## Linking pieces (Fugenelemente)

Sometimes a little connector gets wedged between the parts — most often **-s** or **-(e)n**:

> Arbeit**s**zimmer (study) · Sonne**n**schein (sunshine) · Hund**e**hütte (doghouse)

There's no clean rule for when these appear — just recognize them as glue and don't let them throw
you. They don't change the last-word-wins logic.

---

## Reading long compounds

Split from the **right**:

> Donau · dampf · schiff · fahrt → Donaudampfschifffahrt
> = Danube-steam-ship-travel = "Danube steamboat trip"

Find the final noun (*die Fahrt*) → the whole thing is feminine. Then read the modifiers backward to
unpack the meaning. The length is theatrical; the structure is simple.
