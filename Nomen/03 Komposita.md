# Komposita (compound nouns)

German builds new nouns by **combining existing ones**. The meaning is usually literal — a long
compound is often two or three known words joined together ([[03 Free Wins]]):

> **Hand** + **Schuh** → der Handschuh — "hand-shoe" = glove
> **Haus** + **Tür** → die Haustür — "house-door" = front door

This note covers the **grammar** of compounding.

---

## The last noun is the head

In a compound, the **final noun is the head**. It determines two things:

- the **gender** (the article), and
- the **plural**.

Everything before it is a **modifier**. You do not learn a compound's gender or plural separately —
both are inherited from the last element:

| compound | last element | → gender & plural |
| -------- | ------------ | ----------------- |
| die Haus**tür** | die Tür | die Haustür → die Haustüren |
| das Garten**haus** | das Haus | das Gartenhaus → die Gartenhäuser |
| der Hand**schuh** | der Schuh | der Handschuh → die Handschuhe |

> *der* Saft + *die* Flasche → **die** Saftflasche *(feminine, because Flasche wins)*

Read the word right-to-left, find the final element, and its grammar is the whole compound's grammar.

---

## Linking pieces (Fugenelemente)

A connector sometimes appears between the parts — most often **-s** or **-(e)n**:

> Arbeit**s**zimmer (study) · Sonne**n**schein (sunshine) · Hund**e**hütte (doghouse)

There is no reliable rule for when these occur; recognize them as connectors. They do not affect the
head rule.

---

## Reading long compounds

Split from the **right**:

> Donau · dampf · schiff · fahrt → Donaudampfschifffahrt
> = Danube-steam-ship-travel = "Danube steamboat trip"

Find the final noun (*die Fahrt*) → the whole compound is feminine. Then read the modifiers backward
to determine the meaning.
